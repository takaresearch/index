# New Referral: Diagnostic + Non-Surgical - Patient Communication

## a. History Taking

### Opening
- Good morning/afternoon. My name is Dr. Taka Ogawa. Thank you for coming in today.
- What brings you in? What's the main problem you're experiencing?

### Chief Complaint (Functional Focus)
- What is the main thing that's bothering you?
- What activities have become difficult for you?
- How far can you walk before it becomes a problem?
- Are stairs difficult? Does it affect your sleep?

### Timeline
- When did this start?
- Was there a specific injury or event that triggered it?
- Has it been getting better, worse, or staying about the same?

### Aggravating/Relieving Factors
- What makes it worse? Walking, standing, certain movements?
- What makes it better? Rest, medication, specific positions?
- Is it worse in the morning, during the day, or at night?

### Prior Treatment
- Have you tried any treatments for this?
- What have you done so far? Physiotherapy, medication, injections?
- How long did you try that? Did it help at all?

---

## b. Examination

### Preparation
- Now, let me take a look at your [FOOT/ANKLE]. I'm going to examine the area, check the movement, and see where the pain is.
- Please let me know if anything hurts or if you feel uncomfortable.

### During Examination
- I can see some swelling here.
- There's tenderness when I press here.
- The range of motion is a bit limited.
- The alignment looks [normal / slightly off].

---

## c. Impression

### Diagnostic Explanation
- Based on what you've told me and what I found on examination, I believe you have [DIAGNOSIS].
- Essentially, this means [SIMPLE EXPLANATION IN PLAIN LANGUAGE].

**Example Explanations**:
- **Plantar fasciitis**: This is inflammation of the thick band of tissue on the bottom of your foot. It's a common cause of heel pain.
- **Achilles tendinopathy**: This is a problem with the tendon at the back of your heel. It can become painful and swollen, often due to overuse.
- **Ankle instability**: The ligaments that hold your ankle stable have been stretched or damaged, which can cause the ankle to feel unstable.

### Differential
- There are a few possibilities we need to consider. The most likely is [DIAGNOSIS], but we should also rule out [ALTERNATIVE].

---

## d. Recommendation

### Investigation
- I'd like to order [X-RAY / MRI / ULTRASOUND] to get a better picture of what's going on.
- This will help us confirm the diagnosis and plan the best treatment.
- The results should be available in about [TIMEFRAME].

### Conservative Treatment
- I recommend we start with non-surgical treatment. This includes [TREATMENT OPTIONS].
- Let's try [PHYSIOTHERAPY / ORTHOTICS / MEDICATION] for [DURATION] and see how you respond.
- The goal is to [REDUCE PAIN / IMPROVE FUNCTION].
- It may take [TIMEFRAME] to see improvement, so please be patient.

### Follow-up Plan
- I'd like to see you back in [TIMEFRAME] to review the test results and see how you're doing.
- If the symptoms improve, we'll continue with the current treatment.
- If they persist or get worse, we'll need to consider other options like injections or possibly surgery.
