# Flatfoot (PTTD) - Surgical Management

## Indications for Surgery

- Failed conservative treatment (minimum 3-6 months)
- Progressive deformity
- Stage II or greater disease
- Young, active patients
- Inability to tolerate bracing
- Significant functional limitation

## Surgical Decision Making by Stage

### Stage I: Tenosynovitis Only
**Procedures:**
- PTT debridement and synovectomy
- FDL tendon transfer (if >50% PTT damage)
- Gastrocnemius recession (if contracture present)

**Outcomes:**
- 80-90% good results
- Rare to require this (usually treated non-operatively)

### Stage IIA: Flexible Deformity, Minimal Uncovering
**Procedures (à la carte approach):**
1. **Medial displacement calcaneal osteotomy (MDCO)** - Cornerstone
2. **FDL tendon transfer** - Replace dysfunctional PTT
3. **Gastrocnemius recession** - If contracture (>10° equinus)
4. ± **Lateral column lengthening** (if severe abduction)

**Rationale:**
- MDCO: Shifts ground reaction force medially, decreases PTT demand
- FDL transfer: Restores inversion strength
- Gastrocnemius recession: Decreases valgus moment

**Outcomes:**
- 80-90% good to excellent results
- Patient satisfaction high
- Preserves motion

### Stage IIB: Flexible Deformity, Significant Uncovering (>40%)
**Procedures:**
1. **Medial displacement calcaneal osteotomy (MDCO)**
2. **FDL tendon transfer**
3. **Lateral column lengthening (Evans or Cotton osteotomy)**
   - Evans: Calcaneal osteotomy, opens lateral column
   - Cotton: Medial cuneiform opening wedge
4. **Gastrocnemius recession** (if contracture)

**Evans vs. Cotton:**
- **Evans**: More powerful correction, addresses forefoot abduction
- **Cotton**: Plantarflexes forefoot, addresses dorsiflexion of 1st ray

**Outcomes:**
- 75-85% good results
- More extensive surgery = longer recovery
- Risk of lateral overload pain

### Stage III: Fixed (Rigid) Deformity
**Procedures:**
1. **Double or triple arthrodesis**
   - Double: Talonavicular + subtalar fusion
   - Triple: Add calcaneocuboid fusion
2. ± **Gastrocnemius recession**

**Rationale:**
- Cannot correct with osteotomies alone
- Arthritis often present
- Sacrifices motion but relieves pain

**Outcomes:**
- 70-85% pain relief
- Loss of hindfoot motion
- Gait alterations
- Risk of adjacent joint arthritis

### Stage IV: Valgus Ankle Arthritis
**Procedures:**
1. **Deltoid ligament reconstruction** (if ankle stable)
2. **Ankle arthrodesis or total ankle replacement**
3. **Tibia-talus-calcaneus fusion** (pantalar fusion)

**Outcomes:**
- Salvage procedures
- Variable outcomes
- Significant functional limitation

## Detailed Surgical Techniques

### Medial Displacement Calcaneal Osteotomy (MDCO)
- Lateral incision
- Oblique osteotomy 1-1.5 cm posterior to posterior facet
- Shift posterior fragment medially 8-10 mm
- Fix with 1-2 screws

### FDL Tendon Transfer
- Medial incision
- Harvest FDL through separate plantar incision
- Transfer to navicular
- Secure with interference screw or suture anchor
- Provides dynamic inversion support

### Lateral Column Lengthening (Evans)
- Lateral incision
- Osteotomy through anterior calcaneus
- Insert structural graft (10-12 mm)
- Fix with plate or staples
- Corrects forefoot abduction

### Gastrocnemius Recession
- Posteromedial incision
- Release gastrocnemius aponeurosis (Strayer)
- Or intramuscular recession (Baumann)
- Check for neutral dorsiflexion

## Combination Surgery Algorithm

```
Stage IIA (Mild):
└─ MDCO + FDL transfer + Gastrocnemius recession

Stage IIB (Severe):
└─ MDCO + FDL transfer + Evans + Gastrocnemius recession

Stage III (Rigid):
└─ Double or Triple arthrodesis ± Gastrocnemius recession

Stage IV (Ankle involved):
└─ Pantalar fusion or Ankle fusion + hindfoot fusion
```

## Postoperative Protocol

### Joint-Sparing Procedures (Stage I-II)
**Weeks 0-6:**
- Short leg cast or splint
- Non-weight bearing
- Elevation

**Weeks 6-8:**
- CAM boot
- Begin protected weight-bearing

**Weeks 8-12:**
- Progressive weight-bearing
- Physical therapy
- ROM exercises

**Months 3-6:**
- Return to regular shoes with orthotic
- Continue strengthening
- Return to activities at 4-6 months

### Arthrodesis Procedures (Stage III-IV)
**Weeks 0-6:**
- Short leg cast
- Non-weight bearing
- May need longer (up to 8-10 weeks)

**Weeks 6-12:**
- CAM boot
- Progressive weight-bearing once fusion evident

**Months 3-12:**
- Return to activities at 6 months
- Full recovery 12 months

## Complications

### General
- Wound healing problems
- Infection
- DVT
- Sural nerve injury (lateral procedures)
- Tibial nerve injury (medial procedures)

### Specific to Procedures
**MDCO:**
- Nonunion (rare)
- Under/overcorrection

**Evans:**
- Lateral column overload (5-10%)
- Calcaneocuboid arthritis
- Graft resorption

**FDL Transfer:**
- Loss of great toe flexion strength (usually minimal)
- Transfer failure

**Arthrodesis:**
- Nonunion (5-15% triple arthrodesis)
- Malunion
- Adjacent joint arthritis
- Hardware irritation

## Outcomes by Procedure

| Procedure | Success Rate | Return to Activity | Patient Satisfaction |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| MDCO + FDL (IIA) | 80-90% | 4-6 months | 85-90% |
| MDCO + FDL + Evans (IIB) | 75-85% | 6-9 months | 75-85% |
| Double/Triple arthrodesis (III) | 70-85% | 9-12 months | 70-80% |

## Key Surgical Principles

1. **Address all components**: Tendon, bony alignment, contracture
2. **Stage-appropriate surgery**: Don't overtreat or undertreat
3. **Gastrocnemius contracture**: Common and must be addressed
4. **Lateral column lengthening**: Reserve for significant forefoot abduction
5. **Preserve motion** when possible (joint-sparing procedures)
6. **Arthrodesis**: For rigid deformity and arthritis only

## Revision Surgery

- Often needed if:
  - Inadequate initial correction
  - Progression of deformity
  - Persistent symptoms
- May require conversion to arthrodesis
