    Morton's Neuroma - Surgical Management¶

    Indications for Surgery¶

    • Failed conservative treatment (minimum 3-6 months)
    • Significant pain limiting activities of daily living
    • Failed corticosteroid injections (at least 1-2 attempts)
    • Progressive symptoms
    • Unable to modify footwear adequately
    • Diagnostic nerve block provides relief (confirms diagnosis)

    Preoperative Considerations¶

    Confirm Diagnosis¶

    • Diagnostic nerve block: Should provide significant relief
    • If no relief → reconsider diagnosis
    • Rule out alternative diagnoses (metatarsalgia, Freiberg's, MTP synovitis)

    Patient Selection¶

    • Realistic expectations
    • Willing to comply with postoperative restrictions
    • Appropriate footwear expectations

    Imaging¶

    • Weight-bearing X-rays to rule out other pathology
    • MRI or ultrasound may help confirm neuroma and size

    Surgical Options¶

    1. Neurectomy (Excision) - Gold Standard¶

    Indications¶

    • Standard treatment for refractory Morton's neuroma
    • Most predictable results

    Approach Options¶

    A. Dorsal Approach (Most Common) - Advantages: - Better visualization - Can excise nerve more proximally - Avoids plantar scar (no weight-bearing pain from scar) - Less risk of plantar fat pad injury

    • Disadvantages:
    • Must release deep transverse metatarsal ligament
    • Slightly longer dissection

    Technique: - Longitudinal incision between metatarsals (dorsal) - Retract extensor tendons - Divide deep transverse metatarsal ligament - Identify and trace nerve proximally - Excise neuroma with 1-2 cm of normal nerve proximally - Leave nerve end in intermetatarsal space (not subcutaneous)

    B. Plantar Approach - Advantages: - Direct access to neuroma - Don't need to release ligament

    • Disadvantages:
    • Plantar scar: Risk of painful scar with weight-bearing
    • Less popular for this reason

    Technique: - Transverse or longitudinal incision between MT heads (plantar) - Identify and excise neuroma - Ensure adequate proximal excision

    Preferred: Most surgeons favor dorsal approach due to avoiding plantar scar.

    Outcomes¶

    • Success (good to excellent): 75-90%
    • Symptom relief: 80-85%
    • Recurrence/persistent pain: 10-25%
    • Permanent numbness: 100% (expected, usually not bothersome)
    • Painful stump neuroma: 5-10%
    • Return to activity: 6-12 weeks

    2. Nerve Decompression (Neurolysis)¶

    Indications¶

    • Desire to preserve sensation
    • Patient preference to avoid numbness
    • Early/mild neuromas

    Technique¶

    • Release deep transverse metatarsal ligament only
    • Decompress nerve without excising
    • May combine with metatarsal osteotomy if excessive weight-bearing

    Outcomes¶

    • Less predictable than neurectomy
    • Success: 50-70%
    • Preserves sensation (advantage)
    • Higher recurrence rate than excision

    Disadvantages¶

    • Less reliable pain relief
    • May need later neurectomy

    Generally less favored than neurectomy due to lower success rate.

    3. Cryoablation¶

    Indications¶

    • Alternative to neurectomy
    • Minimally invasive option

    Technique¶

    • Percutaneous or open approach
    • Freeze nerve with cryoprobe (-50 to -70°C)
    • Causes Wallerian degeneration

    Outcomes¶

    • Success: 60-80% (variable in literature)
    • Less invasive
    • May require repeat treatment
    • Limited long-term data

    4. Radiofrequency Ablation¶

    Technique¶

    • Heat ablation of nerve
    • Can be done percutaneously

    Outcomes¶

    • Limited evidence
    • Not widely used

    Decision Making: Neurectomy vs. Decompression¶

    Factor Neurectomy Decompression
    Success rate 75-90% 50-70%
    Predictability High Lower
    Sensation Permanent numbness Preserved
    Recurrence Lower Higher
    Revision if fails Difficult Convert to neurectomy
    Recommendation Gold standard Alternative option

    Most surgeons favor neurectomy due to higher success rate and predictability.

    Postoperative Protocol¶

    Neurectomy (Dorsal Approach)¶

    Weeks 0-2: - Surgical shoe or CAM boot - Weight-bearing as tolerated - Elevation, ice - Dressing changes

    Weeks 2-4: - Progress to stiff-soled shoe - Increase activity - Sutures removed at 2 weeks

    Weeks 4-6: - Transition to regular shoe (wide toe box) - Avoid narrow shoes, high heels

    Weeks 6-12: - Gradual return to activities - Full return to activity 8-12 weeks

    Plantar Approach¶

    • Similar protocol
    • May limit weight-bearing more initially (protect incision)
    • Scar massage after healing

    Complications¶

    Intraoperative¶

    • Injury to adjacent structures (digital arteries, nerves)
    • Incomplete excision

    Early Postoperative¶

    • Wound healing problems (3-5%)
    • Infection (1-2%)
    • Hematoma

    Late Postoperative¶

    • Persistent pain (10-20%):
    • Incorrect diagnosis
    • Incomplete excision
    • Adjacent neuroma (different interspace)

    • Stump neuroma

    • Permanent numbness (100%, expected):

    • Adjacent toes (e.g., 3rd and 4th toes for 3rd interspace)
    • Usually not bothersome

    • Counsel preoperatively

    • Stump neuroma (5-10%):

    • Painful neuroma at proximal cut end

    • May require revision surgery

    • Plantar scar pain (10-20% if plantar approach):

    • Painful with weight-bearing

    • Reason to favor dorsal approach

    • Transfer metatarsalgia (5%):

    • If ligament released, metatarsals may splay

    • Recurrence (5-15%)

    Revision Surgery¶

    Indications¶

    • Persistent or recurrent pain after neurectomy
    • Stump neuroma
    • Missed additional neuroma

    Workup¶

    • Confirm diagnosis with nerve block
    • MRI or ultrasound to identify stump neuroma
    • Rule out other causes (stress fracture, metatarsalgia)

    Options¶

    • Re-excision with proximal dissection:
    • Trace nerve more proximally
    • Excise farther proximal to get beyond scar tissue

    • Bury nerve stump in muscle (interosseous)

    • Neuromas buried in bone: Limited evidence

    • Targeted muscle reinnervation: Emerging technique
    • Nerve capping devices: Limited evidence

    Outcomes¶

    • Less predictable than primary surgery
    • 50-70% success rate

    Outcomes by Approach¶

    Approach Success Rate Numbness Plantar Scar Recurrence
    Dorsal neurectomy 75-90% Yes (expected) No 10-15%
    Plantar neurectomy 70-85% Yes (expected) Yes (risk) 10-15%
    Decompression 50-70% No No 20-30%
    Cryoablation 60-80% Variable No Variable

    Key Surgical Principles¶

    1. Dorsal approach preferred: Avoids plantar scar
    2. Adequate proximal excision: Get 1-2 cm of normal nerve
    3. Confirm diagnosis preoperatively: Nerve block should provide relief
    4. Neurectomy > Decompression: Higher success rate
    5. Counsel about numbness: Permanent, usually not bothersome
    6. Leave nerve stump deep: Not subcutaneous (avoid painful stump)
    7. Multiple neuromas: Address all symptomatic interspaces

    Special Considerations¶

    Multiple Neuromas¶

    • Can address multiple interspaces simultaneously
    • Higher recurrence rate
    • More extensive numbness
    • Counsel patients appropriately

    Failed Prior Surgery¶

    • Stump neuroma vs. incorrect diagnosis
    • Diagnostic nerve block critical
    • Revision surgery less predictable

    Athletes¶

    • Return to sports 8-12 weeks
    • Numbness usually not functionally significant
    • High success rate with surgery

    Patient Counseling¶

    Expectations¶

    • Pain relief: 80-85% good to excellent
    • Numbness: Permanent, expected (between affected toes)
    • Return to activity: 8-12 weeks
    • Footwear: Still need to avoid very narrow shoes, high heels
    • Recurrence: 10-15% may have persistent symptoms

    Decision Making¶

    • Surgery if failed 3-6 months conservative treatment
    • High success rate (75-90%)
    • Numbness is trade-off for pain relief
    • Most patients satisfied despite numbness

    Key Points¶

    1. Neurectomy (dorsal approach) is gold standard
    2. 75-90% success rate with neurectomy
    3. Permanent numbness is expected and usually acceptable
    4. Dorsal approach preferred over plantar (avoids weight-bearing scar)
    5. Adequate proximal excision reduces stump neuroma risk
    6. Decompression has lower success rate, less predictable
    7. Confirm diagnosis with nerve block before surgery
    8. Most patients satisfied despite permanent numbness
