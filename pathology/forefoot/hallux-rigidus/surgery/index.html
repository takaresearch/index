    Hallux Rigidus - Surgical Management¶

    Indications for Surgery¶

    • Failed conservative treatment (minimum 3-6 months)
    • Significant pain limiting activities of daily living
    • Progressive deformity and stiffness
    • Inability to maintain adequate footwear
    • Grade 2-4 disease (especially Grade 3-4)

    Preoperative Assessment¶

    Patient Factors¶

    • Age and activity level
    • Functional demands
    • Shoe wear requirements
    • Occupation (effects of fusion vs. arthroplasty)
    • Expectations: Motion preservation vs. pain relief

    Radiographic Assessment¶

    • Grade (0-4): Guides procedure selection
    • Dorsal osteophytes: Size and location
    • Joint space: Amount of remaining cartilage
    • Cartilage distribution: Dorsal vs. plantar/central
    • Sesamoid arthritis
    • Metatarsal length: Relative to 2nd MT

    Surgical Options by Grade¶

    Grade 1-2: Joint-Preserving Procedures¶

    1. Cheilectomy (Dorsal Osteophyte Excision)¶

    Indications: - Grade 1-2 disease - Dorsal impingement pain - Preserved joint space (>50%) - Dorsal/dorsolateral cartilage loss only

    Technique: - Dorsal incision - Excise dorsal 20-30% of metatarsal head - Remove dorsal osteophytes - Dorsal capsulectomy - Decompress joint

    Outcomes: - Success: 70-90% good to excellent results - Pain relief: 80-90% - Increased dorsiflexion: 20-30° - Durability: 5-10 years average - Revision rate: 20-30% within 10 years

    Advantages: - Preserves motion - Low morbidity - Quick recovery (6-8 weeks) - Can convert to arthrodesis later

    Disadvantages: - Does not address advanced arthritis - Temporary solution for many - May not relieve midrange pain (Grade 4)

    2. Cheilectomy + Moberg Osteotomy¶

    Indications: - Grade 2 disease - Need greater dorsiflexion - Plantar cartilage preserved

    Technique: - Cheilectomy as above - Dorsal closing wedge osteotomy of proximal phalanx - Extends hallux (increases functional dorsiflexion)

    Outcomes: - Similar to cheilectomy, may provide additional dorsiflexion - 75-85% good results

    Grade 2-3: Consider Cheilectomy or Arthrodesis¶

    Decision depends on: - Amount of remaining cartilage - Patient age and activity - Presence of midrange pain

    Grade 3-4: Arthrodesis or Arthroplasty¶

    1. MTP Arthrodesis (Gold Standard for Advanced Disease)¶

    Indications: - Grade 3-4 disease - Young, active patients - Manual laborers - Revision after failed cheilectomy or arthroplasty

    Technique: - Medial or dorsal incision - Prepare joint surfaces (remove cartilage) - Position: 10-15° dorsiflexion, 10-15° valgus, neutral rotation - Fixation: Plate, screws, or combination

    Positioning Critical: - Dorsiflexion: 10-15° relative to floor when standing - Too much: Difficulty walking downhill, IPJ stress - Too little: Difficulty with toe-off, shoe wear - Valgus: 10-15° (match contralateral if normal) - Rotation: Neutral (nail points upward)

    Outcomes: - Fusion rate: 90-95% - Pain relief: 90-95% excellent - Satisfaction: 85-95% - Return to activity: 4-6 months - Durability: Lifelong - Complications: Nonunion (5-10%), malposition, IPJ arthritis

    Advantages: - Excellent, predictable pain relief - Durable solution - Allows return to high-impact activities - No implant issues

    Disadvantages: - Loss of MTP motion - Difficulty with certain shoe wear (high heels) - Stress transfer to IPJ (may develop arthritis) - Permanent solution (not reversible)

    2. Hemi-Arthroplasty (Interposition or Implant)¶

    Indications: - Grade 3-4 disease - Older, lower-demand patients - Desire to preserve motion - Not suitable for high-impact activities

    Types: - Metatarsal head resurfacing (HemiCAP) - Silicone implant (Historical, high failure rate) - Cartiva (SCI) - Synthetic cartilage implant

    Outcomes: - Variable results: 60-80% good to excellent - Preserves some motion - Less predictable than arthrodesis - Revision rate higher than arthrodesis

    Advantages: - Preserves motion - Reversible (can convert to arthrodesis)

    Disadvantages: - Less predictable outcomes - Implant-related complications - Not for high-demand patients - May require revision

    3. Total Joint Replacement (Rare)¶

    Indications: - Very limited - Older, low-demand patients - Rheumatoid arthritis - Desire motion preservation

    Outcomes: - High failure rate (30-50% within 10 years) - Loosening, subsidence common - Generally NOT recommended

    4. Keller Resection Arthroplasty (Salvage Only)¶

    Technique: - Resect proximal phalanx base (30-50%) - Create fibrous pseudoarthrosis

    Indications: - Salvage procedure only - Elderly, low-demand patients - Contraindication to arthrodesis

    Outcomes: - Poor push-off strength - Cock-up deformity - Transfer metatarsalgia - Generally avoided in modern practice

    Decision Algorithm¶

    Grade 1-2 + Preserved cartilage:
└─ Cheilectomy (± Moberg)

Grade 2-3 + Moderate cartilage loss:
├─ Young, active → Cheilectomy
└─ Older, low-demand or midrange pain → Consider arthrodesis

Grade 3-4:
├─ Young, active, high-demand
│  └─ MTP Arthrodesis (Gold Standard)
│
├─ Older, low-demand, want motion
│  └─ Hemi-arthroplasty or Arthrodesis
│
└─ Rheumatoid, very low-demand
   └─ Consider implant arthroplasty

Failed Cheilectomy:
└─ MTP Arthrodesis

    Postoperative Protocol¶

    Cheilectomy¶

    Weeks 0-2: - Surgical shoe - Weight-bearing as tolerated - Elevation

    Weeks 2-6: - Progress to regular shoe (stiff-soled) - ROM exercises immediately

    Weeks 6-8: - Return to activities

    Months 3-6: - Full activities

    MTP Arthrodesis¶

    Weeks 0-6: - Short leg cast or CAM boot - Non-weight bearing (some protocols allow heel weight-bearing) - Elevation

    Weeks 6-8: - CAM boot, protected weight-bearing - Check X-rays for fusion progress

    Weeks 8-12: - Full weight-bearing in boot - Progressive activity

    Months 3-6: - Transition to regular shoe (stiff-soled) - Return to activities at 4-6 months - Full recovery 6-12 months

    Arthroplasty¶

    Weeks 0-2: - Surgical shoe - Protected weight-bearing

    Weeks 2-6: - Weight-bearing as tolerated - ROM exercises

    Weeks 6-12: - Progress activity - Return to normal shoe

    Complications¶

    Cheilectomy¶

    • Recurrence (20-30% within 10 years)
    • Persistent pain (10-20%)
    • Stiffness
    • Neurovascular injury
    • Progression to arthrodesis needed

    MTP Arthrodesis¶

    • Nonunion (5-10%): Most common complication
    • Malposition (5%): Too much/little dorsiflexion or valgus
    • IPJ arthritis (10-20% long-term): Stress transfer
    • Hardware irritation (5-10%)
    • Infection (2-5%)
    • Persistent pain despite fusion (rare)

    Arthroplasty¶

    • Implant loosening/subsidence (10-30%)
    • Osteolysis (silicone synovitis if silicone)
    • Infection (2-5%)
    • Stiffness or persistent pain
    • Revision surgery needed (15-30%)

    Outcomes by Procedure¶

    Procedure Success Rate Motion Durability Patient Satisfaction Return to Activity
    Cheilectomy (Grade 1-2) 70-90% Improved 5-10 years 80-85% 6-8 weeks
    MTP Arthrodesis 90-95% Lost Lifelong 85-95% 4-6 months
    Hemi-arthroplasty 60-80% Partial Variable 70-80% 3-4 months

    Key Surgical Principles¶

    1. Grade determines procedure: Don't do cheilectomy for Grade 4
    2. MTP arthrodesis is gold standard for advanced disease (Grade 3-4)
    3. Positioning in arthrodesis is critical: 10-15° dorsiflexion, 10-15° valgus
    4. Cheilectomy for early disease (Grade 1-2), allows conversion to arthrodesis later
    5. Arthroplasty has higher revision rate, less predictable than arthrodesis
    6. Total joint replacement generally not recommended
    7. Patient selection: Match procedure to patient demand and expectations

    Revision Surgery¶

    Failed Cheilectomy¶

    • Convert to arthrodesis (most common)
    • Outcomes of arthrodesis after cheilectomy are good

    Nonunion after Arthrodesis¶

    • Revision arthrodesis with bone graft
    • Ensure proper positioning
    • Consider biologic augmentation (BMP)

    Failed Arthroplasty¶

    • Convert to arthrodesis
    • More technically demanding
    • May need bone graft
    • Outcomes less predictable than primary arthrodesis

    Special Considerations¶

    Athletes¶

    • Cheilectomy preferred if early disease (preserve motion)
    • Arthrodesis allows return to high-impact sports
    • Some elite athletes successfully compete with arthrodesis

    High Heels¶

    • Difficult with arthrodesis (fixed position)
    • Counsel patients preoperatively
    • Consider arthroplasty if high heel wear essential (though less reliable)

    Bilateral Disease¶

    • Common (up to 80%)
    • Stage surgeries 3-6 months apart
    • Consider doing less affected side first

    Key Points¶

    1. Cheilectomy for early disease (Grade 1-2) with preserved joint space
    2. MTP arthrodesis is gold standard for advanced disease (Grade 3-4)
    3. Arthrodesis provides excellent pain relief (90-95%) with predictable outcomes
    4. Arthroplasty less reliable, higher revision rate
    5. Positioning in arthrodesis is critical for good outcome
    6. Motion vs. Pain relief: Arthrodesis sacrifices motion but gives best pain relief
    7. Failed cheilectomy converts well to arthrodesis
