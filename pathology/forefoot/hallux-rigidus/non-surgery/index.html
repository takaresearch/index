    Hallux Rigidus - Non-Surgical Management¶

    Clinical Presentation¶

    • Pain at 1st MTP joint, especially with dorsiflexion
    • Stiffness and limited range of motion
    • Dorsal prominence ("dorsal bunion")
    • Pain with push-off during gait
    • Difficulty with inclines, stairs
    • Worse with high heels or flexible shoes

    Classification: Coughlin and Shurnas¶

    Grade 0: Dorsiflexion 40-60°, Normal X-ray¶

    • Early symptoms
    • No radiographic changes

    Grade 1: Dorsiflexion 30-40°¶

    • Mild osteophytes
    • Minimal joint space narrowing
    • Subchondral sclerosis

    Grade 2: Dorsiflexion 10-30°¶

    • Moderate osteophytes
    • Flattening of metatarsal head

    • 25% joint space narrowing

    • Sesamoid hypertrophy

    Grade 3: Dorsiflexion <10°¶

    • Severe osteophytes
    • Marked joint space narrowing or loss
    • Cysts, loose bodies
    • May have plantar/lateral cartilage preserved

    Grade 4: Symptoms as Grade 3 + Pain at Midrange¶

    • Pan-articular arthritis
    • Loss of all cartilage surfaces

    Etiology¶

    • Idiopathic (most common): Primary osteoarthritis
    • Trauma: Fracture, chronic turf toe injuries
    • Inflammatory arthritis: Gout, RA, seronegative arthropathies
    • Biomechanical: Long 1st metatarsal, pes planus
    • Osteochondral defect
    • Iatrogenic: Post-surgical (overcorrection in bunion surgery)

    Diagnosis¶

    Physical Examination¶

    • Range of Motion:
    • Dorsiflexion (normal 60-80°)
    • Plantarflexion (normal 20-30°)

    • Pain at end-range dorsiflexion

    • Dorsal Osteophytes: Palpable prominence

    • Grind Test: Axial compression with rotation → pain
    • Pain Pattern:
    • End-range dorsiflexion pain (Grades 1-3)

    • Midrange pain (Grade 4)

    • Gait: Antalgic, decreased push-off

    • Shoe Wear: Look for wear pattern (lateral shift)

    Imaging¶

    • Weight-bearing AP and Lateral Foot X-rays
    • Lateral View (most important):
    • Dorsal osteophytes
    • Joint space narrowing
    • Metatarsal head flattening

    • Subchondral cysts

    • Oblique View: Assess medial/lateral joint surfaces

    • Sesamoid View: Hypertrophy, arthritis

    Non-Surgical Treatment¶

    Indications¶

    • Grades 0-2 (primary treatment)
    • Grade 3 (may provide relief, but surgery often needed)
    • Grade 4 (limited benefit)
    • Low-demand patients
    • Medical comorbidities precluding surgery

    Treatment Goals¶

    • Reduce pain
    • Maintain or improve function
    • Slow progression (uncertain)

    Treatment Options¶

    1. Footwear Modification (Most Effective)¶

    • Stiff-soled shoes: Limit MTP dorsiflexion
    • Athletic shoes with firm sole
    • Hiking boots

    • Stiff dress shoes

    • Rocker-bottom sole: Facilitates roll-through without MTP motion

    • Can be added to existing shoes

    • Carbon fiber inserts: Rigidify shoe sole

    • Low heel height: Reduce dorsiflexion demand

    • Wide toe box: Accommodate dorsal prominence

    Avoid: - Flexible sneakers - High heels - Thin-soled shoes

    2. Orthotic Management¶

    • Morton's extension: Rigid plate under 1st ray
    • Dancer's pad: Offload 1st MTP joint
    • Custom orthotics: May help if biomechanical factors
    • Accommodative padding: Over dorsal prominence

    3. Activity Modification¶

    • Avoid activities requiring extreme dorsiflexion
    • Modify sports participation (running → cycling/swimming)
    • Use appropriate footwear for activities

    4. Pharmacological Management¶

    • NSAIDs: Oral or topical
    • Acetaminophen
    • Topical analgesics

    5. Injection Therapy¶

    • Corticosteroid Injection:
    • Temporary relief (3-6 months)
    • Can repeat 2-3 times

    • Risk: Accelerate cartilage degeneration

    • Hyaluronic Acid (Visco-supplementation):

    • Mixed evidence

    • May provide relief in early stages

    • Technique: Inject dorsally or medially, avoid plantar (sesamoids)

    6. Physical Therapy¶

    • Range of Motion: Maintain mobility
    • Strengthening: Intrinsics, extrinsics
    • Modalities: Ice, ultrasound
    • Gait training: Optimize mechanics

    What DOESN'T Work¶

    • Chiropractic manipulation: No evidence
    • Toe spacers: Irrelevant for this condition
    • Supplements (glucosamine, chondroitin): No proven benefit

    Success Rates¶

    • Grade 1-2: 70-80% improve with footwear modification
    • Grade 3: 30-50% adequate relief
    • Grade 4: <20% benefit
    • Injections: Temporary relief, may delay surgery

    Patient Education¶

    Key Points¶

    • Progressive condition: May worsen over time
    • Non-operative treatment: Manages symptoms, doesn't reverse arthritis
    • Footwear critical: Stiff, rocker-bottom shoes most effective
    • Surgery: Consider if conservative treatment fails
    • Timing: No urgency for surgery unless significant disability
    • Natural history: Variable progression, some remain mild for years

    When to Consider Surgery¶

    • Failed conservative treatment (minimum 3-6 months)
    • Significant pain limiting activities
    • Difficulty with work or daily activities
    • Progression despite treatment
    • Grade 3-4 disease

    Follow-up¶

    • Reassess at 3-6 months
    • Serial X-rays if symptomatic progression
    • Ensure proper footwear and orthotic use
    • Discuss surgery if symptoms persist or worsen

    Special Considerations¶

    Gout¶

    • May present acutely as gout attack at 1st MTP
    • Treat underlying hyperuricemia
    • Higher risk of rapid progression

    Athletes¶

    • May significantly limit performance
    • Consider cheilectomy (surgical) earlier to preserve motion

    Inflammatory Arthritis¶

    • More aggressive arthritis
    • Address underlying disease
    • Often need surgery earlier

    Bilateral Disease¶

    • Common (up to 80%)
    • Stage surgery if both need intervention

    Key Points¶

    1. Footwear modification is the most effective non-surgical treatment
    2. Stiff-soled, rocker-bottom shoes are the gold standard
    3. Injections provide temporary relief, may delay surgery
    4. Grade 1-2 often respond well to conservative treatment
    5. Grade 3-4 usually eventually require surgery
    6. Non-operative treatment doesn't reverse arthritis
    7. Surgery should be considered if quality of life significantly affected
