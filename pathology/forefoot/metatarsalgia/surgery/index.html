    Metatarsalgia - Surgical Management¶

    Indications for Surgery¶

    Surgery is RARELY first-line for primary metatarsalgia

    Indications: - Failed conservative treatment (minimum 6-12 months) - Identifiable structural pathology: - Hallux valgus causing transfer metatarsalgia - Hallux rigidus limiting 1st ray function - Rigid hammer/claw toes - Plantar plate tear - Freiberg's disease - Significant metatarsal length discrepancy - Pes cavus with excessive forefoot loading - Significant functional limitation - Unable to tolerate conservative management

    Important: Surgery addresses the underlying cause, not just the symptom of forefoot pain.

    Preoperative Assessment¶

    Identify the Cause¶

    • Primary vs. secondary metatarsalgia
    • Associated deformities: Hallux valgus, hammer toes, cavus foot
    • Specific pathology: Plantar plate tear, Freiberg's disease
    • Biomechanical factors: Gastrocnemius contracture, metatarsal length

    Radiographic Assessment¶

    • Weight-bearing X-rays
    • Metatarsal parabola: Normal cascade of decreasing length from 1-5
    • Relative metatarsal lengths
    • Associated deformities (bunion, pes cavus)
    • Freiberg's disease (flattening, sclerosis of MT head)

    Advanced Imaging¶

    • MRI: Plantar plate tear, stress fracture, soft tissue pathology
    • CT: Bone detail if needed

    Patient Selection¶

    • Realistic expectations
    • Understanding that pain may not completely resolve
    • Willing to continue orthotic use postoperatively

    Surgical Options¶

    1. Address Associated Deformities (Most Common)¶

    Hallux Valgus Correction¶

    • If bunion present: Correcting bunion often relieves transfer metatarsalgia
    • Restore 1st ray weight-bearing
    • Procedure depends on bunion severity (see Hallux Valgus section)

    Hallux Rigidus Treatment¶

    • If 1st MTP arthritis: Cheilectomy or arthrodesis
    • Restore 1st ray push-off
    • Reduces load on lesser metatarsals

    Hammer/Claw Toe Correction¶

    • Flexible: PIPJ arthroplasty, flexor-to-extensor transfer
    • Rigid: PIPJ arthrodesis
    • Reduces pressure under MT heads

    Gastrocnemius Recession¶

    • If gastrocnemius contracture (>10° equinus)
    • Decreases forefoot pressure
    • Can be combined with other procedures

    2. Metatarsal Osteotomies (For Primary Metatarsalgia)¶

    Indications: - Metatarsal length discrepancy - Prominent MT head - Failed conservative treatment without other deformity

    Weil Osteotomy (Most Common)¶

    Technique: - Dorsal longitudinal incision - Oblique osteotomy parallel to weightbearing surface - Slide MT head proximally (shortens MT) - Fix with screw

    Outcomes: - Success: 70-85% - Complications: Stiffness (20-40%), floating toe, recurrence

    Advantages: - Reliable shortening - Stable fixation

    Disadvantages: - High stiffness rate - Transfer metatarsalgia to adjacent MT (if too much shortening) - Floating toe deformity

    Distal MT Osteotomy (Dorsiflexion)¶

    • Elevate MT head (plantarflex distal fragment)
    • Offloads pressure
    • Less commonly done than Weil

    BRT (Bösch) Osteotomy¶

    • Distal oblique osteotomy
    • Shortens and elevates MT head
    • Alternative to Weil

    3. Plantar Plate Repair¶

    Indications: - MTP instability - Positive drawer test - MRI confirmation of tear

    Technique: - Dorsal or plantar approach - Reattach plantar plate to base of proximal phalanx - Often combined with Weil osteotomy - May need K-wire temporary fixation

    Outcomes: - 70-85% good to excellent results - Improved stability

    4. Freiberg's Disease Treatment¶

    Options: - Early (Stage I-II): Debridement, drilling, Weil osteotomy - Advanced (Stage III-IV): Osteochondral grafting, MT head resection, interpositional arthroplasty

    Technique depends on stage (see specific Freiberg's protocols)

    5. Partial MT Head Resection (Rarely Done)¶

    Technique: - Resect plantar condyles of MT head

    Disadvantages: - Destabilizes joint - High complication rate - Generally avoided

    6. Pes Cavus Correction¶

    If severe pes cavus with excessive forefoot loading: - Calcaneal osteotomy: Dorsiflexion/lateralizing - 1st MT dorsiflexion osteotomy - Plantar fascia release - Complex reconstruction (see Pes Cavus section)

    Decision Algorithm¶

    Cause of Metatarsalgia:

├─ Hallux Valgus (Transfer Metatarsalgia)
│  └─ Bunion correction (Lapidus, osteotomy)
│
├─ Hallux Rigidus
│  └─ Cheilectomy or MTP arthrodesis
│
├─ Hammer/Claw Toes
│  └─ PIPJ arthroplasty or arthrodesis
│
├─ Plantar Plate Tear
│  └─ Plantar plate repair ± Weil osteotomy
│
├─ Freiberg's Disease
│  └─ Debridement ± Weil osteotomy or grafting
│
├─ Metatarsal Length Discrepancy
│  └─ Weil osteotomy (shorten long MT)
│
├─ Gastrocnemius Contracture
│  └─ Gastrocnemius recession
│
└─ Pes Cavus
   └─ Calcaneal/metatarsal osteotomies

    Surgical Principles¶

    Which Metatarsals to Operate On¶

    • Typically 2nd and/or 3rd MT most symptomatic
    • Restore metatarsal parabola (cascade)
    • Avoid creating transfer metatarsalgia

    Amount of Shortening (Weil Osteotomy)¶

    • Usually 3-5mm
    • Too much → transfer metatarsalgia, floating toe
    • Too little → persistent pain
    • Consider 1-2mm less shortening than preoperatively planned

    Avoid Over-Treatment¶

    • Don't shorten multiple metatarsals excessively
    • Risk of transfer lesion or generalized forefoot pain

    Postoperative Protocol¶

    Isolated Weil Osteotomy¶

    Weeks 0-2: - Surgical shoe with stiff sole - Heel or midfoot weight-bearing - Avoid direct forefoot pressure - Elevation, ice

    Weeks 2-6: - Continue surgical shoe - Progressive weight-bearing - Toe ROM exercises (combat stiffness)

    Weeks 6-8: - Transition to wide, stiff-soled shoe - Full weight-bearing

    Months 3-6: - Return to normal activities - Continue orthotic use

    With Bunion/Hammer Toe Correction¶

    • Follow protocol for primary procedure
    • Usually more restrictive initially

    With Plantar Plate Repair¶

    Weeks 0-6: - Surgical shoe or CAM boot - Non- or protected weight-bearing - K-wire if placed (remove at 4-6 weeks)

    Weeks 6-12: - Progressive weight-bearing - ROM exercises

    Complications¶

    Weil Osteotomy Specific¶

    • Stiffness (20-40%): Most common complication
    • Early ROM exercises critical

    • May be permanent

    • Floating toe (5-15%): Toe doesn't contact ground

    • Due to over-shortening or extensor tightness

    • Functional but cosmetic issue

    • Transfer metatarsalgia (5-10%): Pain shifts to adjacent MT

    • Due to over-shortening

    • Recurrence (10-15%)

    • Nonunion (rare, <5%)
    • Malunion

    General Complications¶

    • Infection (2-5%)
    • Wound healing problems
    • Nerve injury (numbness)
    • Persistent pain despite surgery (10-20%)
    • Scar tissue
    • Complex regional pain syndrome (rare)

    Outcomes¶

    By Procedure¶

    Procedure Success Rate Satisfaction Complications
    Bunion correction (for transfer metatarsalgia) 80-90% 85% Specific to bunion surgery
    Weil osteotomy 70-85% 70-80% Stiffness 20-40%, floating toe 5-15%
    Hammer toe correction 75-90% 80% Specific to procedure
    Plantar plate repair 70-85% 75% Stiffness, recurrence
    Gastrocnemius recession 70-80% 75% Sural nerve injury, weakness

    General Outcomes¶

    • Patient satisfaction: 70-85%
    • Pain improvement: Most improve, but complete resolution uncommon
    • Orthotic use: Often still needed postoperatively
    • Return to activity: 3-6 months

    Key Surgical Principles¶

    1. Surgery addresses the cause, not just the symptom
    2. Most commonly: Correct associated deformities (bunion, hammer toes)
    3. Isolated metatarsal osteotomy: Reserved for structural abnormality
    4. Weil osteotomy: Reliable but high stiffness rate
    5. Don't over-shorten: Risk of transfer metatarsalgia, floating toe
    6. Early ROM exercises: Critical to prevent stiffness
    7. Set realistic expectations: May not completely resolve pain
    8. Orthotics often still needed postoperatively

    Revision Surgery¶

    Indications¶

    • Recurrence
    • Transfer metatarsalgia
    • Floating toe (if symptomatic)
    • Malunion/nonunion
    • Persistent pain

    Options¶

    • Revision osteotomy
    • Correct adjacent metatarsals
    • Address missed pathology
    • Arthrodesis (salvage)

    Outcomes¶

    • Less predictable than primary surgery
    • 50-70% success rate

    Special Considerations¶

    Multiple MT Involvement¶

    • May need to address multiple metatarsals
    • Higher risk of complications
    • More prolonged recovery

    Older Patients¶

    • Consider less aggressive surgery
    • Continue orthotic management
    • Surgery if severe disability only

    Athletes¶

    • May need surgery earlier if competitive
    • Return to sports 4-6 months
    • May not return to pre-injury level

    Rheumatoid Arthritis¶

    • Pan-MT head involvement common
    • May need more extensive surgery
    • Resection arthroplasty or arthrodesis
    • Address 1st ray (bunion, hallux rigidus) simultaneously

    Key Points¶

    1. Surgery for metatarsalgia primarily addresses underlying cause, not isolated forefoot pain
    2. Most common surgeries: Bunion correction, hammer toe correction (for secondary metatarsalgia)
    3. Weil osteotomy for primary metatarsalgia or metatarsal length discrepancy
    4. High stiffness rate with Weil (20-40%) - early ROM critical
    5. Set realistic expectations: Complete pain resolution uncommon
    6. Orthotics often needed even after surgery
    7. Success rate: 70-85% overall, depends on cause
    8. Consider surgery only after 6-12 months failed conservative treatment
