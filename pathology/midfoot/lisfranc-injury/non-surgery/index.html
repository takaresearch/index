    Lisfranc Injury - Non-Surgical Management¶

    Clinical Presentation¶

    • Midfoot pain and swelling
    • Difficulty weight-bearing
    • Plantar ecchymosis (high suspicion for Lisfranc injury)
    • Often from low-energy mechanism (twist, fall)
    • High-energy trauma in MVA, crush injuries

    Anatomy¶

    • Lisfranc Joint Complex: Tarsometatarsal (TMT) joints
    • Lisfranc Ligament: Strongest, connects medial cuneiform to 2nd metatarsal base
    • Keystone: 2nd metatarsal base recessed, provides stability
    • Ligament Injury: Leads to instability and deformity

    Classification¶

    Mechanism¶

    • Low-energy: Twist injury, fall (indirect)
    • High-energy: MVA, crush (direct)

    Myerson Classification¶

    • Type A: Total incongruity (all TMT joints)
    • Type B: Partial incongruity
    • B1: Medial (1st TMT)
    • B2: Lateral (2nd-5th TMT)
    • Type C: Divergent (medial and lateral separation)

    Stability¶

    • Stable: <2mm displacement, no diastasis
    • Unstable: >2mm displacement, any diastasis

    Diagnosis¶

    Physical Examination¶

    • Plantar Ecchymosis: Pathognomonic sign
    • Midfoot Swelling: Diffuse
    • Tenderness: Over TMT joints
    • Piano Key Test: Dorsoplantar stress causes pain, instability
    • Abduction Stress: Reproduces pain
    • Passive Forefoot Pronation/Supination: Pain with stress

    Imaging¶

    • Weight-bearing X-rays (Essential if possible)
    • AP, lateral, oblique foot
    • Non-weight-bearing X-rays: May miss subtle instability
    • Key Findings:
    • Diastasis between 1st and 2nd metatarsal (>2mm)
    • Loss of alignment at TMT joints

    • Fractures of metatarsal bases or cuneiforms

    • CT Scan: Better visualization of fractures, joint subluxation

    • MRI: Assess Lisfranc ligament integrity, occult fractures
    • Weight-bearing CT: Gold standard for subtle instability

    Suspicious Findings Requiring Further Workup¶

    • Fleck sign (avulsion fracture)
    • Any diastasis between 1st and 2nd metatarsal
    • Loss of alignment at TMT joints
    • Plantar ecchymosis

    Non-Surgical Treatment¶

    Indications (RARE - Most require surgery)¶

    • No diastasis (<2mm)
    • Anatomic alignment on weight-bearing X-rays
    • No fracture displacement
    • Stable injury pattern
    • Low-demand patient

    Treatment Protocol¶

    Phase 1: Acute Immobilization (0-6 weeks)¶

    • Short leg cast
    • Non-weight bearing
    • Strict compliance required
    • Ice, elevation

    Phase 2: Progressive Loading (6-12 weeks)¶

    • Repeat X-rays at 6 weeks
    • If stable: CAM boot, protected weight-bearing
    • Continue non-weight bearing if any displacement

    Phase 3: Return to Activity (3-6 months)¶

    • Gradual weight-bearing
    • Custom orthotic with arch support
    • Strengthening program
    • Carbon fiber insert or rigid sole shoe

    Critical Monitoring¶

    • Serial X-rays: Weekly for first 3 weeks, then at 6 weeks
    • Weight-bearing X-rays at 6 weeks: Essential to confirm stability
    • Any displacement: Convert to surgery immediately

    Patient Education¶

    Key Points¶

    • High risk of displacement: Even "stable" injuries may displace
    • Importance of compliance: No weight-bearing, frequent X-rays
    • Threshold for surgery: Any displacement >2mm requires surgery
    • Long-term implications: Risk of post-traumatic arthritis
    • Arch support: Lifelong need for arch support/orthotics
    • Return to sports: May take 6-12 months

    Warning Signs¶

    • Increased pain
    • Feeling of instability
    • Unable to maintain non-weight bearing
    • → Immediate X-ray needed

    Outcomes of Non-Surgical Treatment¶

    • Success Rate: Variable, many studies show high failure rate
    • Risk of Displacement: 15-30% may displace even with casting
    • Post-traumatic Arthritis: Can occur even with anatomic reduction
    • Functional Outcomes: Generally worse than surgical treatment for unstable injuries

    When to Convert to Surgery¶

    Absolute Indications¶

    • Any displacement >2mm on follow-up X-rays
    • Persistent pain despite immobilization
    • Patient non-compliance with non-weight bearing
    • Athlete or high-demand patient

    Relative Indications¶

    • Even "stable" injuries may benefit from surgery
    • Low threshold for surgical fixation

    Follow-up Protocol¶

    • Week 1-2: X-rays, assess for displacement
    • Week 3: X-rays
    • Week 6: Weight-bearing X-rays (critical)
    • Week 12: X-rays, assess healing
    • Thereafter: Monitor for arthritis development

    Important Caveats¶

    Low Threshold for Surgery¶

    • Many experts recommend surgery for ALL Lisfranc ligament injuries
    • Non-operative treatment only for:
    • Isolated cuneonavicular injuries
    • Pure midfoot sprains without ligamentous disruption
    • Truly stable injuries confirmed on weight-bearing X-rays

    Subtle Instability¶

    • Standard X-rays may miss instability
    • Weight-bearing imaging essential
    • CT or MRI if clinical suspicion high

    Key Points¶

    1. Lisfranc injuries are often UNSTABLE → Most require surgery
    2. Non-operative treatment reserved for truly stable injuries only
    3. Serial X-rays mandatory to detect displacement
    4. Weight-bearing X-rays at 6 weeks are critical
    5. Low threshold for converting to surgical treatment
    6. Plantar ecchymosis = high suspicion for Lisfranc injury
    7. Long-term arch support needed regardless of treatment
